In May 2021, I graduated from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill with highest distinction. I received a B.S. in economics (game theory; auctions), and designed my own, second major, “Applied Physics,” which concerned the numerical methods that can explain and derive natural phenomena. I also earned a minor in Latin, focusing on reader-response theory, African-American reception, and Propertius.

I earned placement into Phi Beta Kappa, and was an Honors Carolina Laureate, Robert E. Bryan Fellow, Launch Chapel Hill Fellow, 1789 Venture Fellow, and a few others.

I am particularly enabled to explore many different things because of my enrollment in the Morehead-Cain program. The first merit scholarship in the U.S., it accepts 3% of applicants, and fully funds a four-year educational experience, including summer enrichment, personal discovery, and outdoor leadership. Because of the program, I have been able to study classics in Manchester, poetry in Rome, biking in California, among other things.

We launched UNCUT in September 2019. Since then, we’ve added added 28 team members, amassed 100K+ content views and 6K+ followers across seven platforms, and have had content featured on ESPN, Bleacher Report, the Sports Shop Radio, (a second time with Terrell Owens, Tim Brown, and myself, but that was taken down), and more.

Wage is a scheduling software merging machine learning and economic theory to solve inefficiences in the workplace.

I started Wage in June 2018, and presented the company at Uber Headquarters in April 2019. The software incorporates various elements of welfare economics and labor, social choice, and auction theory. The purpose of the app is to let employees schedule themselves, and in turn, making them and the employers happier than they would be otherwise.

Bolt Bikes (now Zoomo) offers electric bikes that optimize delivery time for food couriers.

I spent a few weeks of the hectic, historic summer of 2020 as a software engineer/economics associate, with a main focus on bike security. I saved $14K in bike hardware, updated software protocol, and biked around SF.

Carbon is a digital manufacturing company using 3D printers to bring ideas to market faster.

I spent the summer of 2019 at Carbon, where I worked on some cool data projects, explored San Francisco, and 3D printed a replica 12th century Lewis chessmen set.
