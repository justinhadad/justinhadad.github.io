Things They Don't Tell You About London

Oct 25, 2021

In March 2020 (everything that follows this preposition is typically sad; this is no exception) I received funding for a trip to ITER, a nuclear fusion research plant based in Saint-Paul-lez-Durance, France. I roped in my friend Stef, a maths student from St Andrews who studied abroad at UNC in 2019. Obviously the trip never happened.

We had been trying to see each other for years, but couldn't quite nail it (or beat COVID): she planned two separate trips to the US, I tried going to Cambridge for a research trip to translate Latin, etc. So in fall 2021, when my Swiss permit was delayed but I still had my flight to London, I thought, why not go?

For my first week of travels, I'm on Stef's living room couch, in east London's Stepney Green.

One of my more frequent musings concerns information dissemination. Everyone knows a lot of things, ranging topically from the certain subjects they enjoy, popular culture, hack-y ways to go about life, and so on. Many people assume that others know these things; others feel inclined to share this knowledge; others may feel privy to that knowledge and have some incentive to keep it to themselves. I personally am not at a stage in my life where I feel even remotely motivated to share the things I know (I'm 22, I know nothing), but I am aware that there are [edge] cases where people may benefit from my disclosure of the things I've learned.

I've been totally floored by a few idiosyncracies. Here are some things that have struck me.

Hot Sauce

Story one: As we youth often do, I was at a McDonald’s far past bedtime the other day. I ordered chicken nuggets with *sour cream and chive dip* because everything else was far out of my taste buds’ range (this should say something). I went to the counter and asked the cashier for a hot sauce. “This is a McDonald’s” she said. “Why the **** would we have hot sauce?”

Story two: Stef and I went to The Yurt so that I could try the English-famous “bubble and squeak,” an absurd mix of leftovers. They were out of whatever goes in bubble and squeak, so I got a kidney bean and lentil soup instead. Of course, I wanted hot sauce, so I went to the cashier and asked for some. “There’s mustard to your left,” he said.

Offies

Open carrying is chill here, so people often buy tinnies (beer) from offies (off-license stores, as opposed to "on-licenses" where drinks must be consumed on the premise). Beer and pint are synonyms, and can (should) be consumed at ridiculously early hours.

Guns exist

This one is absolutely my fault. I was walking from north to east London very late into the night. My head was down, mindlessly scrolling away while I absolutely rinsed Lorde’s “Stoned at the Nail Salon” (it was a down bad kind of night). I saw an orange dot at the center of my jacket between my eyes and my phone, and looked up to see a ski-masked person holding *something that looked like a gun* with both hands. He was probably six or so feet away.

I immediately booked it in the other direction, running faster than I thought was possible through all kinds of alleyways. (This was probably a bad idea.) To the lectures of literally everybody I’ve told about this, walking home that late won’t happen again.

I thought it was impossible––aren’t guns banned here?––but there have been more than 20,000 incidents of gun violence over the last decade.
